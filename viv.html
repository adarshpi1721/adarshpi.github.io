Suppression of Vortex-Induced Vibrations (VIV)

Image Placeholder

Summary — This project looks at passive techniques to suppress vortex-induced vibrations of cylinders. I helped build a low-speed wind tunnel facility, validated it with airfoil and cylinder tests (accuracy up to ~96%), and developed an OpenFOAM solver to simulate 2D VIV. The goal was to pair experiments and simulations and test small passive add-ons that reduce oscillation amplitudes.

Approach

We combined experimental rig-building with CFD. The experiments collected time-resolved transverse displacement and force data, while the simulations reproduced the same inflow and geometric parameters to test candidate suppressors — e.g., splitter plates, small control cylinders, and passive roughness patterns downstream. The solver was tuned against experimental benchmarks first, then used for parameter sweeps.

Key findings

Several passive devices helped reduce vibration amplitude in certain Strouhal ranges. Splitter-plates worked reliably when sized and placed correctly, while small downstream control cylinders sometimes delayed lock-in. The numerical solver matched experiment trends closely enough to be useful for design screening — which saved time and allowed us to test more configurations than would be feasible experimentally.

Challenges & lessons

Accurately capturing the vortex dynamics requires careful time-stepping and mesh resolution; coarse setups can entirely miss lock-in behavior. The main practical lesson was the value of combining experiments and simulations — experiments revealed real-world nonlinearities (structural damping, support friction) and simulations allowed fast exploration of geometric parameter space.

What I learned

This project improved my experimental skills (wind-tunnel setup, instrumentation) and my CFD tuning abilities (meshing, solver validation). It also sharpened my engineering judgment about the trade-offs between suppression efficacy, structural complexity, and manufacturability.
