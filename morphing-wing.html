Morphing Foils — Biomimicry of Fish Locomotion

Summary — This project merges two ideas: using trailing-edge morphing to actively tune flapping-foil kinematics, and understanding how fish use body flexibility to shape their wake. I built ALE-based 2D models to simulate morphing–pitching–heaving motion and explored how timing (phase), amplitude, and morph rate influence thrust, power, and wake structure. The main finding was that well-timed morphing can increase thrust by ~31% and efficiency by ~12%.

Approach

I developed a deliberately simple but physically interpretable ALE 2D model. The steps were:

  • Define a baseline pitching–heaving motion, then add morphing as an extra degree of freedom.
  • Run parametric sweeps on morph timing (phase), amplitude, and rate.
  • Analyze wake dynamics using DMD to see which modes strengthen or weaken propulsion.
  • Compare morphing runs against the non-morphing baseline to quantify gains.

Key findings

Morphing effectively reshapes the vortex pairing process, creating stronger reverse von Kármán wakes when timed correctly. Poorly timed morphing weakens thrust, so performance is extremely phase-sensitive. The strongest cases showed higher wake coherence and significantly improved force production.

What I learned

This project taught me how sensitive unsteady propulsion is to phase and geometry, and how small kinematic changes can completely reshape the wake. I also learned how to balance numerical modeling with physical intuition and how to use DMD to understand the “why” behind thrust improvements.
