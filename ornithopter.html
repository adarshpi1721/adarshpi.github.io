Ornithopter

Image Placeholder

Summary — This project started as an attempt to build a controllable flapping-wing aircraft and turned into a practical lesson in taking FSI ideas into hardware. I led the design of a dual-crank mechanism to generate bird-like flapping and later added a simple morphing mechanism at the trailing edge to improve thrust and stability.

Approach

We first focused on getting a robust mechanical system: repeatable flapping kinematics, acceptable structural stiffness, and a reasonable weight budget. Once the basic ornithopter flew, I experimented with small morphing elements at the wing trailing edge and adjusted flapping frequency and amplitude to see how they affected thrust and control.

Key observations

Even small changes in flexibility and timing made a noticeable difference in flight stability and climb capability. The project highlighted how sensitive flapping systems are to inertia, stiffness, and timing, and how control is tightly coupled to aerodynamics.

What I learned

This is where my interest in biomimicry and unsteady aerodynamics really began. It taught me how different real hardware feels compared to idealized models, and how important it is to design mechanisms and experiments that are simple enough to debug but rich enough to show interesting physics.
